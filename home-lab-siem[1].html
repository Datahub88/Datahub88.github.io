Building a SOC on a Laptop: The Home Lab SIEM

I wanted hands-on proof I could operate a SIEM, not just talk about one in an interview. So I built one — Wazuh, a couple of VMs, and two live attacks I ran against myself.

The setup

The lab runs on VirtualBox: a Wazuh 4.14.5 server as the monitoring hub, a Kali Linux box to play attacker, and a Windows 10 VM as the target. Wazuh agents installed on the endpoints ship logs back to the server, where I built out detection rules and dashboards.

Attack simulation 1 — Nmap SYN scan

First test was reconnaissance: an Nmap SYN scan from the Kali box against the Windows target. The goal wasn't just "does Wazuh see it" — it was building the muscle of going from raw alert to a documented incident.

Attack simulation 2 — Hydra brute force

Second test was a Hydra brute-force attempt against RDP. This one generates a much noisier signal — repeated failed auth attempts — and let me tune detection thresholds instead of just eyeballing logs.

Writing it up like a real case

The part that actually mattered for my GRC goals wasn't the attacks — it was the paperwork after. I wrote formal incident reports (IR-001 and IR-002) for both events, each one mapping the activity to:

  • MITRE ATT&CK techniques observed
  • NIST 800-53 control families
  • PCI DSS, HIPAA, ISO 27001, and SOC 2 relevance

That mapping exercise is the actual skill GRC and analyst roles want: turning a technical event into something an auditor, a regulator, or a compliance team can act on.

An incident isn't done when the attack stops. It's done when someone who wasn't in the room can read your report and understand what happened, why it matters, and what control should exist because of it.

What's next

IR-003 is in the queue — a privilege escalation scenario, which will round out the detection-to-response story with something post-compromise instead of just perimeter noise.

Full repo, configs, and both incident reports are on GitHub: Home-Lab-SIEM.
